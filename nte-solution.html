S.T.A.S.H - an NTE Proposal by Team UAE

Short Term Alginate Semi-Permeable Hydrogel to Preserve Genetic Material

The Challenge: Biological Sample Preservation

In many regions, especially those with extreme climates like deserts, the rapid and safe transport of biological samples (DNA, RNA, other biological matter) to laboratories poses a significant challenge. Traditional methods often fail under harsh conditions, leading to degradation and compromised research or diagnostic results.

Existing solutions like FTA cards offer some stability but can be insufficient for preserving delicate RNA or larger biological structures over extended periods. Standard cold-chain systems are often cumbersome, expensive, and difficult to maintain in remote or field settings.

Our Innovative Solution: Alginate Biopreservation Pearls

Team UAE has developed a novel approach using **sodium alginate/calcium chloride pearls** for the short-term preservation of DNA, RNA, and other biological matter. These pearls encapsulate the biological material, providing a stable microenvironment that protects against degradation.

Our system is designed to be a superior replacement for FTA cards and conventional cold-chain systems, particularly for field use in extreme desert conditions.

Key Components:

  • Sodium Alginate/Calcium Chloride Pearls: These biocompatible hydrogel beads offer excellent encapsulation and protection for biological samples.
  • 3D Printed Portable Case: A custom-designed field kit, 3D printed from wood PLA, providing a lightweight, durable, and sustainable enclosure for the pearls.
  • PCM Cold Pack Integration: A Phase Change Material (PCM) cold pack is integrated into the case to maintain an optimal temperature of **6-8Â°C for days on end** in hot desert environments, ensuring sample integrity without the need for refrigeration or power.

This solution drastically reduces the risk of sample degradation, making biological research and diagnostics more accessible and reliable, even in the most challenging environments.
